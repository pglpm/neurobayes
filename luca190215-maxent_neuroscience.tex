\section{The problem}
\label{sec:prblem}

A probability distribution is our quantified uncertainty about a set of
mutually exclusive and exhaustive propositions. So when we see a
probability distribution it's natural to ask: \emph{what are we uncertain
  about?} The answer to this question should be precise, not vague as for
example \enquote{the network state}: what network? at what time? To give a
meaningful answer it's useful to specify in detail what experiment would be
done to answer the question with certainty.

Asking the question above is also a good way to distinguish probability
distributions from other distributions. For example, if we're shown a
\emph{frequency} distribution and we ask \enquote{what are we uncertain
  about?}, our question makes no sense.

Together with asking what our uncertainty is about, it's also natural to
ask:
\begin{enumerate*}
\item Which initial assumptions lead to this distribution?
\item Which data lead to this distribution?
\end{enumerate*}

This note tries to answer these questions for probability distribution of
maximum-entropy form appearing in the neuroscientific literature.

\section{First interpretation}
\label{sec:1st_interpretation}

In this first interpretation the maximum-entropy distribution is actually
an approximation. The context is the following. We have a recorded sequence
of neural activities of $N$ neurons, time-binned and binarized:
$\set*{s(t) \suchthat t=1,2,\dotsc; s(t) \in \set{0,1}^N}$. We wonder what
activity we would measure in a \emph{new} time bin, in the past, or in the
future, or some intermediate time bin missing from the data above.

Let's explicitly describe the question, the assumptions, and the data, and
then discuss them in more depth. I'd like you to pay particular attention
to the assumptions: it's rare for them to be explicitly spelled out in the
neuroscientific literature, thus giving the wrong impression that fewer
assumptions are needed.


\medskip

\textbf{The question:} What is the neural activity $s(t')$ in a time bin
$t'$ \emph{outside} of the recorded ones?

\medskip

\textbf{The assumptions:}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textsc{a}\arabic*.,ref=\textsc{a}\arabic*]
\item \label{item:exchangeability}The order of the time bins,
  \emph{including any number of new ones}, is irrelevant for our
  inferences.
\item \label{item:sufficiency}For some biological reason, only the first $R$ moments of observed
  data are \emph{relevant} for the prediction of the activity in a new time
  bin; this is true for any amount of data.
\item \label{item:p_moments}Some assumption that leads to a quantified
  uncertainty about the first $R$ moments of the activities of \emph{every}
  number of time bins (not just the recorded ones).
\item \label{item:p_g}Some assumption that leads to a quantified
  uncertainty about $s(t')$ \emph{if we knew the first $R$ moments} of the
  activities of \emph{every} number of time bins.
\end{enumerate}
Note that the second assumption implies the first.

\medskip

\textbf{The data:} The set of recorded activities $\set{s(t)}$.

\medskip

The assumptions above -- denote them by $A$ -- determine a unique \dob\ for
\emph{every possible} sequence of activities $\set{s(\yt)}$, before the
observation of any data. For simplicity let me consider the case $R=3$:
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:dob_new_activity}
  \begin{gather}
    \begin{multlined}[b][0.9\linewidth]
      \pf\bigl[\set{s(\yt)} \| A\bigr] =
      \int\!\di\ya\;
      \yp(\ya)\times{}\\
      \prod_{\yt}\frac{g[s(\yt)]}{Z(\ya)}\;
      % g(s)\;%\times{}\\
      \exp\bigl[\ya^i s_i(\yt) + \ya^{ij} s_i(\yt)\, s_j(\yt) + \ya^{ijk} s_i(\yt)\, s_j(\yt)\, s_k(\yt)\bigr]\;
    \end{multlined}
    \\\shortintertext{with}
    \label{eq:Z}
    Z(\ya) \defd \sum_s
    g(s)\;
    \exp\bigl(\ya^i s_i + \ya^{ij} s_i s_j + \ya^{ijk} s_i s_j s_k\bigr).
  \end{gather}
\end{subequations}
Einstein's summation convention is used in the formula above, and the sums
run over increasing sequences $i<j<k$. The integral is over
$N+N\,(N-1)/2+N\,(N-1)\,(N-2)/6$ parameters
$\ya\defd (\ya^{i}, \ya^{ij}, \ya^{ijk})$; in general it will be over
$\tbinom{N}{1}+\dotsb+\tbinom{N}{R}$ parameters.

The fact that the assumptions above determine
formula~\eqref{eq:dob_new_activity}, and vice versa, is the discrete
version \citep{fraser1963,andersen1970} of the Koopman-Pitman-Darmois
theorem \citep{koopman1936,pitman1936,darmois1935}[see
also][]{barankinetal1963,denny1967,hipp1974,lauritzen1974,lauritzen1984,lauritzen1982_r1988}.
Assumption~\ref{item:sufficiency} -- the assumption about a sufficient
statistics -- is the one leading to a mixture of exponentials, but it
doesn't determine the density $\yp$ and the distribution $g$. These are
determined by assumptions~\ref{item:p_moments} and~\ref{item:p_g}.

From the distributions~\eqref{eq:dob_new_activity} we can calculate our
\dob\ about any subsequence of activities conditional on any other distinct
subsequence:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dob_new_activity_conditional}
      \pf\bigl[\set{s(t')} \| \set{s(t)}, A\bigr] =
      \frac{\pf\bigl[\set{s(t'), s(t)} \| A\bigr]
      }{
        \pf\bigl[\set{s(t)} \| A\bigr]},
\end{equation}
with numerator and denominator given by~\eqref{eq:dob_new_activity}.

If the data $\set{s(t)}$ comprise a sequence long enough to render the
product of exponentials much more peaked in $ya$ than the density $\yp$,
then the conditional probability~\eqref{eq:dob_new_activity_conditional} is
approximated by a product of maximum-entropy distributions with Lagrange
multipliers $\ya$ determined by the usual constraint equations with data
$\set{s(t)}$ \citep{portamana2017}. Thus the probability
distribution~\eqref{eq:dob_new_activity_conditional} will be approximated
by a product of maximum-entropy distributions.

\medskip

There are several important points about this interpretation that are
rarely mentioned in the neuroscientific literature:
\begin{description}[wide,mode=unboxed]
\item[Which data are \emph{relevant}?] There's a natural question regarding
  assumptions~\ref{item:exchangeability} and \ref{item:sufficiency}: which
  time bins should be considered \emph{relevant} for one another, with
  their particular order still irrelevant? The choice is subjective. We
  could consider, for example, the sequence of activities between minute 1
  and minute 3 of a recording session done on Monday on test subject \#1; or
  the whole of Monday's recording session; or the sequences from Monday's
  and Wednesday's recording sessions; or combined sequences from test
  subjects \#1 and \#4 (hence with different neurons); and so on -- as long
  as every sequence has $N$ neurons. We may give biological reasons for one
  choice and against another; the point is that such a choice is beyond the
  probability calculus. We can of course compare two different choices
  within the probability calculus -- for example to consider only data
  within one recording session, or across multiple sessions -- and then
  calculate their posterior probabilities given some common data. But to do
  this we'd need to make assumptions about what's relevant and what's not
  on a more general level. The subjective choice hasn't disappeared; it has
  just been pushed somewhere else.
\item[Stationarity.] 
\end{description}
